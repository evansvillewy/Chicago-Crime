Classifier Model Comparison

Machine Learning:We examined the ability of three machine learning algorithms to predict whether recorded crimes in the City of Chicago resulted in an arrest. This is a classification problem with a binary answer â€“ arrest or no arrest. We used the official City of Chicago data portal at https://data.cityofchicago.org/ to download crime records for the years 2016-2019. We decided to train our model on information from the years 2016-2018. This provided us with a robust dataset of 787 901 observations. The dataset had 22 features, which were a mix of time, location, and other categorical data. We tested the ability of our models to predict arrests for the 256 908 crimes recorded in 2019. The three models used were a Random Forest (RF) classifier, a Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) classification model, and a linear Support Vector Machine (SVM) model. Feature engineering was done differently in each of the three models, and the features included to predict the target were different. The RF classifier used the following 12 features: month, hour, day of week, district, block, ward, beat, community area, location description, FBI code, primary type, anddomestic. The SGD model used the following 11 features one hot encoded resulting in a total of 100 columns: month, hour, day of week, district, ward, community area, description, location description, fbi code, primary type, and domestic. The SVM model used the following 13 features: month, hour, day of week, district, ward, beat, community area, description, location description, iucr, FBI code, primary type, and domestic.Of the models tested herein, the RF classifier performed best with a weighted average f1-score of 0.85, SGD second best with 0.83 and the SVM model last with 0.69. All three models were much better at labeling crimes that did not result in arrest as non-arrest than those that did result in arrest as arrest. Specifically, all three models classified less than 50% of true arrests as arrests but classified virtually 100% of true non-arrests as non-arrests. Conversely, all three models had weighted average precision greater than 75%, meaning that for all instances classified one way or the other, based on the numerical occurrence of each class, they were correct 75% of the time.


We believe this exercise was a useful examination of the conditions that could help predict the quantity and likelihood of solving crimes given a particular set of characteristics. As reported by the Pew Research Center, (https://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2017/03/01/most-violent-and-property-crimes-in-the-u-s-go-unsolved/) most violent and property crimes in the US go unsolved. Efforts to establish criminal patterns could bolster the efforts of law enforcement in solving crimes and preventing future ones. While our models did notprovide clear evidence for particular conclusions, the importance of feature engineering cannot be underestimated, and the different strengths of each model underscore the need for more work to be done in determining the best combination of features and machine learning algorithms for the task of establishing criminal patterns and arrests.
